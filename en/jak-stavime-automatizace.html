How we do it

Automation that removes repetitive work — not people’s jobs

Retyping orders, searching price lists and repeating forms are jobs for machines. We automate your real processes and systems, with people keeping judgment and control.

Repetitive work doesn't just cost time. It drains capacity and know-how.

These recurring problems come from real companies, with an impact measured in hours and money.

The order arrives by e-mail and someone retypes it

A wholesaler's back office entered orders and attendance by hand, item after item, creating avoidable work and errors.

A process engineer spends hours creating near-identical items

A machine shop manually created 15–25 assemblies, or 70–120 Helios items, every week from drawings and repeated standards.

A salesperson clicks through exports to find a price

Sales searched K2 and Excel for dimension, material, colour and price combinations, risking stale data with every query.

Management signs off, but doesn't know who saw the document

Contract approvals ran through e-mail without an audit trail, leaving management unable to prove who reviewed each document.

Five areas where technology handles routine better than people

We solve five common types of operational bottleneck.

Transactional — data that gets retyped

Extract attachments, sync CRM and ERP, create records and generate quotes — including e-mail-to-order workflows.

Knowledge — answers hidden in documents

Answer questions across PDFs, Excel and drawings in natural language, always with the source.

Reporting — numbers people are waiting on

Bring ERP and warehouse data into one executive view without manual assembly.

Communication — questions that keep coming back

Handle recurring web, e-mail and WhatsApp inquiries 24/7, escalating only what matters.

Monitoring — deadlines and deviations that slip through

Detect deadlines and process deviations early, while leaving decisions to people.

Four phases that protect you from an expensive mistake

We start with today's process cost, then prove each next step on real data.

1. Analysis — measure today

We map inputs, handoffs and duplicate work, then establish time, cost and quality baselines for the business case.

2. Pilot — prove it on real data

A live prototype tests actual documents. For one machine shop, we first audited 5–10 drawings because layout affects reliability and price.

3. Implementation — connect systems and roles

We integrate existing systems, data, approvals and decision points so the solution becomes part of daily operations.

4. Support — operate, measure and tune

We run and improve the solution after launch. One K2 engagement included two years of hosting and a source-code buyout option.

Choosing what to automate — and what to leave to people — takes real expertise

Reliability depends on where automation stops and human judgment remains in control.

We exclude unreliable machine reading

For complex drawings, OCR reads only dependable title-block and bill-of-material fields — not ambiguous dimensions.

What only a person can see stays with a person

An engineer enters sheet thickness or bend count in a PDF dialog; verified data then creates the ERP structure.

Realism is part of the contract

Plans account for ERP-data delays, holidays and client dependencies instead of relying on unrealistic dates.

The system records; the person signs

A manufacturing company: how we built an agent that creates ERP items for the process engineer

Step 1

Starting point: hours of routine every day

Engineers manually created 15–25 assemblies, or 70–120 Helios items, each week from PDF, DWG and DXF files.

Step 2

Initial audit of real drawings

We reviewed 5–10 PDFs before quoting, then piloted one customer with a consistent title block.

Step 3

A deliberate decision: what to read and what not to

OCR reads reliable title-block and bill-of-material fields; complex dimensions remain with the engineer.

Step 4

The agent creates the structure and standards

It finds similar ERP items, creates the component structure and carries over routing and time standards through the Helios API.

Step 5

Human-in-the-loop where the person decides

A PDF dialog captures ambiguous values such as sheet thickness or bend count before verified metadata enters Helios.

Step 6

Phasing and result

Phase one covers manufactured items; phase two adds purchased and standard parts. The engineer now checks only what needs human judgment.
